CHAPTER II

contents.

The most fundamental building blocks of an electronic system are the electrical elements. Each electrical element is connected to the rest of the circuit (system) through their terminals. The most basic electrical elements have two terminals. These terminals serve as the entry and exit points for the flow of electric current.

Some basic examples of two terminal elements are:
battery
resistor
capacitor
diode, etc.
For most of the two terminal elements, the two terminals have polarities. These polarities dictate the direction of electric current flow. Typically, these terminals are denoted by the symbols "+" and "-", signifying positive and negative polarities, respectively.

symbols.

In order to draw the electric circuit diagrams of an electronic system or subsystem, one has to identify all the basic elements. These elements and their connections are then represented graphically by different symbols.

Conductor
A material or substance that allows the flow of electrical current through it with low resistance.
Two conductors crossing but not joined
Two separate conductors that are not electrically connected.
Two conductors joined together
Two conductors that are electrically connected or joined.
Power Supply
A device or system that provides electrical energy to an electrical circuit.
Fixed resistor
A resistor with a predetermined resistance value that cannot be adjusted or changed.
Variable resistor
A resistor whose resistance value can be adjusted or varied manually or by an external control.
Cell
A single electrochemical device that generates electrical energy through chemical reactions.
Battery of 3 cells
A combination of three individual cells connected together to provide a higher voltage or capacity compared to a single cell.
Alternative symbol for battery
A graphical representation used to depict a battery in electrical circuit diagrams or schematics.
Switch
A device used to open or close an electrical circuit, thereby controlling the flow of current.
Filament lamp
A type of electric lamp in which light is produced by the glowing filament (usually made of tungsten) when current passes through it.
Fuse
A protective device that interrupts the flow of current in a circuit by melting when the current exceeds a certain level, thus preventing damage to the circuit.
Ammeter
A measuring instrument used to measure the electric current in a circuit, typically connected in series.
Voltmeter
A measuring instrument used to measure the voltage difference between two points in an electrical circuit, typically connected in parallel.
Indicator lamp
A small lamp used to visually indicate the status of a circuit or device, often by emitting light when activated.
